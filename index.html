⚙️ Target Grammar for Descriptions

To speak confidently about role models, we need these four grammar tools:

1. Defining Relative Clauses with "Who" & "Whose"

Use who to define people and describe their actions, and whose to show possession.

A role model is someone who sets a good example.
A true hero is a person whose actions inspire others to be kind.

2. Past Simple vs. Present Perfect

Use the Past Simple for completed actions in the past with a specific time (e.g., in 2018, when she was a child). Use the Present Perfect for achievements and life experiences without a specific time.

Past Simple: Sümeyye Boyacı won a silver medal in 2018. (Finished time)
Present Perfect: Sümeyye has broken many records in swimming. (Life achievement)

3. Modals of Recommendation: Should / Shouldn't

Use should and shouldn't to talk about what characteristics make a good or bad role model.

Celebrities should behave responsibly because teenagers watch them.
Role models shouldn't brag about their money or success.

Sümeyye Boyacı: Swimming Against the Current

Sümeyye Boyacı was born in 2003 in Eskişehir, Turkey. She was born without arms and with a hip dislocation, but she never let these physical challenges stop her. From an early age, she proved to be incredibly resilient. She learned to write with her foot and began painting beautiful artworks at a young age.

In 2008, after watching fish swim in an aquarium, Sümeyye asked herself a question: "If fish can swim without arms, why can't I?" She started swimming when she was only five years old. It was a major struggle initially, but through sheer determination, she mastered the sport.

Since she began competing, she has achieved outstanding international success. In 2018, she won the gold medal in the 50m backstroke at the European Para Swimming Championships. She has also represented Turkey at the Paralimpik Games.

Sümeyye is a wonderful role model for teenagers worldwide. She has shown that we can overcome obstacles if we believe in our dreams. She is dedicated to helping other disabled young people. Through her life story, she has made a huge contribution to society, inspiring thousands of people to follow their passions.

⚠️ Turkish ➔ English Common Mistakes

Turkish learners often make these grammatical and lexical mistakes when talking about role models. Click each card to understand the correction!

1. Explaining Agreement: "Agree someone" +
❌ I am agree with my mother. / I agree my father.
✓ I agree with my mother. / I agree.
In Turkish, you say "katılıyorum" or "sana katılıyorum". However, in English, "agree" is a verb, not an adjective. So, do NOT say "I am agree". Also, we must use the preposition with when we agree with a person.
2. Double Subject Pronouns +
❌ My role model she is a doctor.
✓ My role model is a doctor. / She is a doctor.
In English, you cannot repeat the subject. "My role model" is the subject, so you do not need "she" immediately after it. Choose one: either the noun or the pronoun, but not both!
3. Saying "According to me" +
❌ According to me, celebrities shouldn't behave badly.
✓ In my opinion, / I think celebrities shouldn't behave badly.
Turkish speakers translate "Bence" literally as "According to me". While grammatically possible, native English speakers use "According to..." for other people or sources (e.g., "According to the news..."). When speaking about your own opinion, use In my opinion, I think, or Personally, I believe.
4. Verb Collocation: "Make a career" +
❌ He made a career in soccer.
✓ He built a career / pursued a career in soccer.
In English, we do not "make" a career. Instead, we use collocations like build a career, have a career, or pursue a career.
5. Relative Clause Double Subject +
❌ A hero is a person who he helps others.
✓ A hero is a person who helps others.
When using relative clauses, the relative pronoun who replaces the subject pronoun (he/she/they). Adding "he" or "she" after "who" is a common double subject error.
